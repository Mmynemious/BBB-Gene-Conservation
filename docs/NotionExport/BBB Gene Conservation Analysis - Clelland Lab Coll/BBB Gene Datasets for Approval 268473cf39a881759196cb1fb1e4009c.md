## Paper 1: Daneman et al. (2010)

> Paper link: https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0013741
> 
- **Main findings**
    - isolated endothelial cells from three mouse tissues (brain, liver, lung)
    - identified 584 genes with higher expression in brain endothelial cells
    - used comparative approach to define BBB-specific gene signature
- **Relevance to the research project**
    - the 584 genes that were expressed higher in endothelial cells could explain why the BBB is restrictive
    - provides vaidated gene sets for further analysis
    - if we would find a similar expression in other species, we could assume that the BBB proteins are evolutionarily conserved & mouse models are valid for human BBB research
- **Additionally**
    - 1000+ citations
    - open-access

## Paper 2: Munji et al. (2019)

> Paper link: [Profiling the mouse brain endothelial transcriptome in health and disease models reveals a core blood-brain barrier dysfunction module - PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6858546/)
> 
- **Main Findings**
    - updated BBB gene catalog using modern RNA-sequencing (confirms + expands Daneman 2010)
    - identified "BBB dysfunction module": these are the same genes which are disrupted across stroke, seizures, MS, and brain trauma
    - understanding of diease mechanisms: BBB breakdown = brain endothelial cells lose BBB identity and revert to peripheral endothelial cell behavior
    - Universal pathway: Despite different disease triggers, all show identical molecular BBB dysfunction pattern
- **Relevance to the research project**
    - provides updated BBB gene list
    - adds disease dimension: analysis of healthy BBB and diseased BBB genes
    - cross-validated Daneman’s research with better tech
    - disease translation question: if these pathways found in mice could predict human BBB pathways, this study of mice can still be worthwhile
- **Additionally**
    - Nature publication
    - methodological consistency
    - Understanding the dysfunction module could lead to pan-neurological disease therapeutics

## Paper 3: Giger et al. (2010)

> Paper link: [Evolution of neuronal and endothelial transcriptomes in primates - PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20624733/)
> 
- **Main findings**
    - neuronal and endothelial cells evolve at different rates in primate brains
    - endothelial genes (ENDEX) are more variable across tissues and development than neuronal genes (NEX)
    - But neuronal genes show more between-species differences than endothelial genes
    
    Some species-specific results:
    
    - **Human vs. Chimpanzee:** Neuronal genes 1.3x more divergent than endothelial
    - **Human vs. Macaque:** Neuronal genes 1.9x more divergent than endothelial
    - **Chimpanzee vs. Macaque:** Neuronal genes 1.5x more divergent than endothelial
    
    → deducting from this paper: BBB endothelial genes are more evolutionarily conserved than brain neuronal genes across primates!
    
- **Relevance to research project**
    - relevant quantitative data
    - could prove that genes are conserved
    - provides baseline conversation rates to compare later against

## Paper 6: Wälchi et al. (2024)

> Paper Link: [Single-cell atlas of the human brain vasculature across development, adulthood and disease | Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07493-y)
> 
- **Main findings**
    - Performed single-cell RNA sequencing analysis of 606,380 freshly isolated endothelial cells
    - Developed a single-cell atlas
    - Identified molecular heterogeneity of the vasculature of healthy fetal and adult human brains and across five vascular-dependent central nervous system (CNS) pathologies
